Why this is still limited
Laser intensity noise, scattered light, and fluorescence can all be modulated at the same frequency — by the beam itself or the sample. The lock-in cannot distinguish these from the real Brillouin signal.

Modulate the pump at 102 kHz and the probe at 2 kHz. Their true interaction creates a mixing product at 100 kHz. No background process generates exactly this beat note — the lock-in tuned to 100 kHz sees only the real Brillouin interaction.

Key insight
Signal lives where noise is absent

The frequency mixing emerges from a simple trig identity. When two modulated beams interact in the sample, their product produces sum and difference frequencies.

sin(2π·102kHz·t) × sin(2π·2kHz·t)
= ½ [ cos(2π·100 kHz·t) − cos(2π·104 kHz·t) ]
Pump alone at 102 kHz
Visible at detector — but so is any scattered pump light, fluorescence excited by pump, etc. Lock-in at 102 kHz would also catch those artifacts.
Probe alone at 2 kHz
Same problem: probe leakage and sample autofluorescence can share this frequency.
Mixing product at 100 kHz — the key
This frequency only appears when pump and probe both interact in the same volume simultaneously. No independent source of noise naturally produces a tone at exactly 100 kHz. Lock-in here → pure Brillouin signal.
Sum product at 104 kHz (discarded)
Also produced by the multiplication but carries the same information. Typically ignored; the difference frequency is used.
Why 100 kHz is a "quiet neighborhood"
Most biological noise (motion, thermal, 1/f drift) lives at low frequencies. Most photon shot noise is broadband. Choosing 100 kHz places the measurement well above the 1/f wall yet far below the detector's bandwidth limit — the noise floor there is as flat and low as it gets.
