Laser Welding
basics, physics & applications

A focused beam of coherent light becomes a heat source so intense it can melt steel in milliseconds. These notes build the intuition first — why a laser can weld at all — then the physics that splits the process into two regimes, the parameters you turn in the lab, and where industry actually uses it. 01 — Introduction

What makes a laser a welding tool

Laser beam welding (LBW) fuses materials by focusing a laser onto a joint until the metal melts and solidifies into a single piece. The laser is not magic — it is simply the most concentrated heat source we can practically aim. The whole subject flows from a single quantity: how many watts you can pack onto a square centimetre.

Why engineers reach for it

  • High energy density → deep, narrow welds at high speed, with very little heat spread into the surrounding part.
  • Low distortion & small heat-affected zone (HAZ) → precision parts stay dimensionally stable; ideal near electronics or thin sheet.
  • Non-contact & single-sided → no electrode wear, no filler required for many joints, access from one side only.
  • Automatable & fast → fiber-delivered beams steer through robots and galvo scanners at metres per second.

Where it sits among welding processes

Every fusion process is really a contest of power density. Crank it up and the weld gets deeper, narrower, and cleaner — until you tip from melting into vaporising.

Oxy-fuel flame~10³ W/cm²Wide, shallow, large HAZ

02 — Laser–material interaction

Getting the light into the metal

Before a laser can heat anything, the surface has to absorb it rather than reflect it. For an opaque metal, absorptivity A = 1 − R where R is reflectivity. This single number decides how much of your expensive laser power actually does work — and it is frustratingly variable.

What absorptivity depends on

  • Wavelength. Metals get shinier at longer wavelengths (Drude behaviour). A 1.07 µm fiber laser couples far better to steel than a 10.6 µm CO₂ laser. Copper and gold are nearly mirrors in the infrared — the reason green (515 nm) and blue (450 nm) lasers now dominate EV-battery copper welding.
  • Temperature. As metal heats, absorptivity climbs — a self-reinforcing effect once heating begins.
  • Surface condition. Oxide layers, roughness, and coatings raise absorption; a polished mirror surface fights you.
  • Angle & polarization. Matter at steep incidence and inside the keyhole walls.
The keyhole's secret weapon

Once the beam drills a vapour cavity (a "keyhole"), light bounces many times off the molten walls before it can escape. Each bounce deposits a little more energy. This multiple-reflection trapping drives the effective absorptivity from maybe 0.30 up to 0.80–0.95 — even for a metal that was shiny when cold. This is why keyhole welding is so efficient, and the simulator below models it directly.

03 — The two regimes

Conduction vs. keyhole — the central idea

Define irradiance (power density) as power divided by focused spot area:

I = P ⁄ (π r²) = P ⁄ (π (d/2)²)   [ W/cm² ]  — smaller spot or higher power ⇒ higher I

Push I past a material-dependent threshold (~10⁶ W/cm² for steel) and the process flips from gently melting to violently vaporising. That flip defines the two welding modes.

Conduction mode I ≲ 10⁶ W/cm²

The surface melts; heat flows downward and outward by conduction. The pool is wide and shallow.

  • Aspect ratio (depth/width) ≲ 1 — a smooth, rounded dish
  • Low spatter, clean cosmetic bead
  • Best for thin sheet, hermetic seals, visible joints
  • Lower coupling efficiency (uses the metal's bare absorptivity)

Keyhole mode I ≳ 10⁶ W/cm²

Intensity vaporises metal; recoil pressure opens a vapour capillary lined with melt. The beam plunges deep.

  • Aspect ratio up to ~10 — deep, narrow "nail" profile
  • Very high coupling (multiple-reflection trapping)
  • Best for thick sections, structural, high speed
  • Risk of porosity & spatter from keyhole instability

The transition itself is simply the point where I reaches the threshold. Everything the lab technician does — choosing power, focus, and speed — is ultimately steering the process across this boundary. Time to make it tangible.

How to read it · for the lecturer

Good in-class demonstrations: (1) hold P and d fixed, raise speed — watch line energy and penetration fall together. (2) Hold P and v, shrink the spot — power density climbs and the weld snaps from dish to keyhole. (3) Switch to copper at infrared and ask why nothing happens until you push the power hard — then introduce green/blue lasers. The model is deliberately simplified (room-temperature properties, energy-balance penetration) so the trends are clear; real welds add fluid flow, plume, and keyhole dynamics on top.

05 — Parameters & governing relations

The dials and what they control

Three numbers dominate practice. Two are independent settings; the third is what you actually care about.

Line (heat) input — the master control

Eℓ = P ⁄ v   [ J/mm ] — energy delivered per millimetre of seam

Line energy governs penetration depth and HAZ size. More power or slower travel ⇒ more energy per millimetre ⇒ deeper weld and a larger heat-affected zone. It is the first knob to reach for.

Power density — sets the regime

I = P ⁄ (π (d/2)²)   [ W/cm² ] — decides conduction vs. keyhole

You raise I by adding power or by tightening the focus (smaller spot, achieved with better beam quality M² or a shorter focal length). Two welds with identical line energy can look completely different if one is focused tightly into keyhole and the other is defocused into conduction.

Interaction time

ti = d ⁄ v   [ s ] — how long any point sees the beam

Penetration scaling (keyhole, simplified)

D ∝ (A · P) ⁄ (v · d · ρ · hm)   hm = cp(Tm − T₀) + Lf (melting enthalpy)

Deeper with more absorbed power, slower travel, and tighter focus; shallower for reflective, high-melting, or high-conductivity metals. This is exactly the relation the simulator integrates.

The melting-efficiency trade-off

Only a fraction of the incident energy ends up as melt enthalpy — the rest is lost to conduction into the bulk, reflection, and the vapour plume. Melting efficiency peaks at intermediate speeds: too slow and heat leaks away by conduction; too fast and the beam couples poorly. The practical sweet spot is "as fast as you can while still reaching the required penetration."

06 — Laser sources

Which laser does the welding

The source sets wavelength (hence absorption), beam quality (hence achievable spot), and delivery. Today the field is overwhelmingly near-1 µm solid-state.

SourceWavelengthStrengthsTypical use
Fiber (Yb)1.07 µmHigh wall-plug efficiency, excellent M², fiber deliveryDominant for metals — automotive, general
Disk (Yb:YAG)1.03 µmVery high power, good beam quality, beam shapingHigh-power keyhole, remote welding
Nd:YAG1.064 µmFlexible, pulsed or CW, legacy installed baseSpot/seam welding, repair
CO₂10.6 µmCheap high power; but mirror delivery & poor metal couplingThick section, non-metals (declining)
Diode (direct)0.9–1.0 µmCompact, efficient, lower brightnessConduction welding, brazing, cladding
Green / Blue515 / 450 nmHigh absorptivity in Cu & Au — stable, low-spatterEV battery copper, electronics
Why colour matters for copper

Copper reflects ~95% of 1 µm light when cold, so infrared welding is unstable — it sputters until the keyhole randomly ignites. At 450 nm copper absorbs several times more, giving a calm, repeatable melt. The rise of EV batteries (thousands of copper tab welds per pack) is the main reason blue and green high-power lasers became a serious product class.

07 — Defects & quality

What goes wrong, and why

  • Porosity. The keyhole is dynamic; if it collapses it traps vapour as a void. Hydrogen porosity plagues aluminium. Mitigation: stable parameters, beam oscillation/wobble, reduced-pressure or vacuum welding.
  • Spatter. Melt ejected by keyhole pressure fluctuations. Worse at high speed and in reflective metals; beam shaping and ramping help.
  • Hot (solidification) cracking. Depends on alloy composition and joint restraint; some alloys are simply crack-sensitive.
  • Lack of fusion / penetration. Too little power density or line energy, or poor joint fit-up — the laser is unforgiving of gaps.
  • Humping & undercut. High-speed instabilities in the melt track.

Modern lines monitor welds in real time with photodiodes, high-speed cameras, acoustic sensors, and optical coherence tomography (OCT), which measures keyhole depth live — closing the loop on penetration control.

08 — Applications

Where laser welding earns its keep

Automotive

Bodies & powertrain

Tailor-welded blanks, body-in-white seams, transmission gears, and galvo "remote" welding at high throughput.

E-mobility

Battery & motors

Cell-to-busbar and tab joints, copper–aluminium connections (green/blue lasers), and hairpin stator welding for EV drives.

Aerospace

Engines & structures

Turbine and combustor components, structural joints, and hybrid laser-arc welding for thick, gap-tolerant sections.

Medical

Implants & devices

Hermetic sealing of pacemakers and sensors, fine surgical-instrument welds, biocompatible titanium joining.

Electronics

Micro-welding

Sensor packaging, connector and lead-frame welds, and precise low-heat joints next to sensitive components.

Heavy industry

Shipbuilding & pipe

Hybrid laser-arc welding of thick plate — keyhole depth plus arc gap-bridging — for fast, low-distortion seams.

09 — Key takeaways

The five things to remember

1

Power density rules. Whether you melt or vaporise — conduction or keyhole — is decided by watts per cm², set by power and focus.

2

Two regimes, two shapes. Conduction = shallow dish, low spatter. Keyhole = deep narrow finger, high efficiency, harder to control.

3

Line energy (P/v) is your master dial for penetration and HAZ; speed and power trade off against each other.

4

Absorptivity is fickle. Wavelength and the keyhole's multiple reflections can swing coupling from 5% to 90% — copper at IR is the cautionary tale.

5

Fiber/disk near 1 µm dominate, with green/blue rising for copper. Match the colour to the metal.
